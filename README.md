# A tipsheet for how to investigate 9-1-1 hold times 

## BACKGROUND:

The National Emergency Number Association is a non-profit, nation-wide organization focused on 9-1-1 operations, technology, education and policy issues. They are NOT a government agency, but they do provide recommended standard operating procedures for the industry. Their 2020 edition of the NENA Standard for 9-1-1 Call Processing outlined a recommendation that 90% of all 9-1-1 calls be answered within 15 seconds and 95% answered within 20 seconds. ( https://drive.google.com/file/d/1LTRQ1vyUoAOYcU2RJ7Zgu-YfVg05K8xc/view?usp=sharing) NENA does not have the power to enforce this standard, but industry professionals use NENA’s standards to measure agency performance.

9-1-1 agencies are typically housed within the municipality’s police departments. See if your state’s office of emergency management (or equivalent) has a map or list of 9-1-1 agencies. Keep in mind that some super tiny police departments will have their own 9-1-1 centers – like colleges and universities. Agencies may span multiple counties in rural areas. In urban areas, you may have both county and city 9-1-1 agencies (the county 9-1-1 center is probably run by the sheriff’s office). If you live within the city limits, the city 9-1-1 agency handles your calls. If you are in an unincorporated part of the county, that’s when the county agency steps in.

You may come across “Next Gen 9-1-1,” which is essentially a nation-wide effort to upgrade 9-1-1 infrastructure. It’s not super relevant for answer time metrics, but it’s good to know that a lot of agencies are in the midst of updating their systems right now.

## HOW TO GET DATA:

Decide how far back you want data. I recommend getting a few years of pre-2020 data if possible because 2020 and 2021’s 9-1-1 call volume was atypical due to the pandemic. The pandemic also led to staffing shortages at many agencies (lots of reasons for this – people wanted to work from home, first-responder burnout, people could get higher paying less stressful jobs elsewhere), so you’ll want to see what the call volume and answer time metrics looked like at the agency before the chaos of the pandemic.

Decide what timeframe you want – weekly, monthly or annual reports. We asked for monthly reports so we could see trends within the year, but we ultimately used annual data for the stories (if you have monthly data, you can add it up to give you annual metrics).

We found that answer time metrics are calculated two ways: with abandoned calls and without abandoned calls. An abandoned call is when someone calls 9-1-1 but hangs up before reaching an operator. That could be after 1 second or it could be after multiple minutes. The agency should have the ability to produce the metrics both ways. We asked for both because if there was a significant difference between the two, it indicated a large number of people were hanging up while on hold. Agencies argued that we should not use the answer time metrics that included abandoned calls because sometimes people accidentally call 9-1-1 or devices make automatic calls when help is not needed (Apple has this feature, as well as some security systems). While that’s fair for calls that are on hold for just a few seconds, someone that hangs up after more than 20 seconds on hold is likely not an accidental call. Which metric you use is a judgement call for you and your editor.

You should also ask for the “abandoned call rate,” which will indicate how often people are hanging up while on hold.

We got what we wanted by asking for “answer time metric reports including call volume, the number and percentage of abandoned calls, and the number and percentage of calls answered broken down by second-increments.” In an ideal world, I recommend you start with a relatively friendly agency and ask for one answer time metric report for one month or one year and ask the PIO or tech person what the export options are and what fields are available.

Some gave us annual data, some gave us monthly data; some included time increments up to 3 minutes, others stopped at 20 seconds; some gave us raw numbers, others just percentages. Standardizing the data so you can compare agencies is key.

For our investigation, we compared the annual 20-second answer rate, excluding abandoned calls. That gave us a metric we could get from each agency, even if they presented the data differently.

### Some strategies for if the agency is not-so-friendly:

* If they say they don’t have any reports by that name – See what your state’s open records law says about agencies producing reports. In Georgia, an agency has to produce a report/document, even if it does not exist already, if doing so would only take a few clicks (like hitting export in the system). See if answer times were ever a topic of conversation at city council or county board meetings – ask for the docs presented at those meetings.

* If they say their system can’t provide metrics – Ask for the name of the software they use to track 9-1-1 agency performance. If they won’t even do that, see if you can find the contract for the software through the municipality’s procurement data or meeting minutes. Then look up the software’s handbook (or request it – the agency should have a manual) and look for export instructions. Send them the instructions for exactly how you’d like the data exported.

* If an agency won’t give you the reports – Ask them for the “20-second answer rate” and see if they can produce that number or if that’s a metric they track at all. If they can’t, and they say answer time metric reports don’t exist, your story is that they are not tracking their performance.

* We had an agency claim that only they could determine what a 9-1-1 call was, so only they could calculate the answer time rate (and they wouldn’t tell us their definition of a 9-1-1 call OR their calculated answer time rate based on this “definition”). They argued that people calling 9-1-1 for non-emergencies (like calling because someone got their order wrong at a restaurant) should not count as 9-1-1 calls and therefore should not be included in their metrics. We pushed back by saying there is an accepted definition of a 9-1-1 call as any call made by dialing 9-1-1.

You should also ask for staffing data, especially if the agency is not meeting the 95% within 20-second standard. You can ask for the number of approved/budgeted positions, the number of positions filled, the number of people in the training pipeline and the number of open positions. We asked for monthly reports going back a year or two. If I could do this investigation again, I would ask for any labor contracts and employee policies (especially leave policies) to see if there was chronic absenteeism.

## IMPACTED COMMUNITY MEMBER SOURCES:

We scoured social media for people expressing frustration with 9-1-1, particularly being placed on hold. We also did a social media call-out, where we asked people to share their experience with the 9-1-1 system.

A word of caution: your brain processes time in a weird way during a traumatic event. Someone could *swear* they were on hold for 15 minutes but in reality it was less than 5. They’re not lying to you – their brain is tricking them. For this reason, ALWAYS get the 9-1-1 and (if relevant) CAD reports for their emergency call(s). To get the report, ask the relevant agency for the call report generated for the call that came from your source’s phone number on the day of their incident. If you don’t know who called 9-1-1, you can also ask for all call details on that day/time at the incident location.

## MORE HELPFUL INFO:

PSAP = Public Safety Answering Point aka a 9-1-1 call center. Depending on the size of the agency, there may be multiple call centers (for example, the Atlanta Hartsfield-Jackson International Airport has its own call center that handles all 9-1-1 calls at the airport)

Operator = The people who answer 9-1-1 calls. Sometimes called “call takers.”

Abandoned = A call that gets hung up or disconnected before speaking to an operator.

Automatic Callback = A feature some 9-1-1 agencies have implemented that automatically calls the number back if the call gets disconnected or the person hangs up. This is another reason agency officials told us to disregard “abandoned calls” when measuring the answer-time rates.

Inbound vs. Outbound Calls = An inbound 9-1-1 call is a person calling 9-1-1. An outbound call from the 9-1-1 center could be a few things. It could be a callback or it could be a call to police, fire or EMS to alert them about an incident. For our analysis, we did not care about outbound calls.

Nuisance calls or non-emergency calls = When someone calls 9-1-1 but does not need emergency services. Agencies told us that these calls are a big problem because they clog up the lines and waste operators' time. We found several agencies were investing in public information campaigns about appropriate agencies to call for non-emergencies  (3-1-1, 4-1-1, 5-1-1, etc) and how to file a police report without calling 9-1-1.

Next-Gen 9-1-1 = Infrastructure that allows people to text 9-1-1, among other improvements. If you want to know more, check out the Next Generation 911 Institute or the Congressional Next Gen 911 Caucus.

Call misrouting = This occurs when a call is routed to the wrong 9-1-1 dispatch center (say you live in Atlanta but your call goes to the DeKalb 9-1-1 agency). This causes delays because the dispatch center is supposed to transfer you to the correct agency so they can handle the call using the appropriate emergency services. Misrouting can be caused by cell tower issues or jurisdictional overlap.

911.gov = The National 911 program, housed within the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration at the U.S. Department of Transportation, provides grant funding and resources to 9-1-1 agencies across the country. They track relevant national legislation, create annual reports, provide recommendations, and currently they are very involved in the transition to Next-Gen.
